Location of DANAE

The current prototype of DANAE is located in Vienna, Austria, at an altitude of about 190 m above sea level, on the ground floor of the laboratory building. The detector is operated inside a vacuum chamber at a pressure of approximately 5×10⁻⁶ mbar (5×10⁻⁴ Pa) and is surrounded by thermal shielding.

The building provides passive shielding: the roof and floors correspond to about 1.2 m of concrete. In addition, the vacuum chamber contributes roughly 1 cm of stainless steel and the thermal shielding about 5 cm of aluminium. These layers are relevant for estimating the expected environmental background.

What are RNDR DePFETs?

A DePFET (depleted p-channel field-effect transistor) is a fully depleted silicon pixel sensor with an integrated charge collection and storage node and an in-pixel first-stage amplification. In contrast to CCDs, which transfer charge to a common readout node, a DePFET provides a dedicated readout transistor in each pixel (active pixel concept).

An RNDR-DePFET enables repetitive non-destructive readout (RNDR) by moving charge between two internal storage nodes (sub-pixels) using a transfer gate. The collected electrons modulate the channel conductivity and thereby the drain current. Charge can be removed via the clear contact in combination with a clear gate (see Fig. 1).

Charge generated in the depleted silicon bulk drifts towards the internal gates. Using the transfer gate, the charge is first stored in one node, e.g. gate 1 and read out. The charge is then moved to gate 2, and gate 1 is read out again to obtain an updated baseline, which is subtracted from the first measurement. The procedure is repeated analogously for gate 2.

By moving the same charges back and forth, the signal can be measured N times without destroying the charge. Averaging reduces the readout noise approximately as 1/√N, which is reflected by the narrowing of the noise peak in Fig. 2.

Dark matter detection

In direct detection experiments, energy depositions in a target material can be observed via different experimental channels, most commonly scintillation light, phonons (heat), and ionization charge (see Fig. 3). DANAE uses the ionization channel: a dark matter particle may scatter off an electron and transfer enough energy to promote it into the conduction band of silicon, producing a measurable charge signal.

To suppress thermally generated charge carriers (dark current), the detector is cooled to about 140 K.

The setup

The setup consists of a vacuum chamber (see Fig. 4) operated at a pressure of about 5×10⁻⁶ mbar. Most electronics are placed outside the chamber to reduce thermal load and electromagnetic interference. The DePFET control and front-end readout electronics, however, are located inside the vacuum to keep cable lengths short and to minimize capacitive pickup and noise.

The sensor comprises a 64×64 matrix of RNDR-DePFET pixels. Rows are read out sequentially while the pixels within a row are read out in parallel. With 800 repetitions per pixel, a full sensor frame is acquired in about 1.2 s (left panel of Fig. 5).

The sensor thickness is 450 μm, and each pixel has a side length of 50 μm. As shown in the right panel of Fig. 5, two sub-pixels form an elongated, oval-like structure.
